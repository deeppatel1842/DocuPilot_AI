## Background and Literature Review

### Retrieval-Augmented Generation in Enterprise Search

RAG systems enhance traditional search by combining retrieval of relevant document segments with generative models that produce coherent, contextually rich responses. This hybrid approach addresses limitations of standalone retrieval or generation, particularly in enterprise contexts where data is voluminous, diverse, and often unstructured.

### Chunking Strategies

Chunking involves segmenting documents into smaller units ("chunks") for embedding and retrieval. The choice of chunking strategy directly influences retrieval quality, semantic coherence, and computational overhead.

- **Fixed-Size Chunking:** Divides text into uniform segments by character or token count. It is computationally efficient but risks splitting sentences or semantic units, degrading retrieval precision and coherence [1][2][3][4].

- **Semantic or Structure-Aware Chunking:** Segments text based on semantic boundaries, such as sentences, paragraphs, or document structure. This approach preserves context and improves recall but increases computational complexity and engineering effort [1][4][5].

- **Hierarchical Chunking:** Organizes chunks in a multi-level structure reflecting document hierarchy, facilitating context-aware retrieval at different granularities [1][5].

- **Metadata-Enriched Chunking:** Incorporates metadata (e.g., document type, author, timestamps) into chunk representations, enhancing retrieval accuracy by providing additional context beyond content alone. This method can improve AI accuracy up to fivefold compared to content-only approaches [1].

- **Recursive Character Splitting with Overlap:** A hybrid method that recursively splits text into overlapping chunks to balance chunk size and semantic coherence. It is considered a strong default for many use cases, offering a practical trade-off between fixed-size and semantic chunking [3][5].

### Reranking Trade-offs

Reranking refines the initial retrieval results to prioritize the most relevant and coherent chunks for generation.

- **List-Aware Reranking-Truncation Models:** Jointly optimize reranking and truncation of candidate lists to balance relevance and computational cost. These models improve retrieval precision but may require complex training and tuning [5].

- **Neural Joint Models:** Employ deep learning architectures to simultaneously rerank and truncate retrieval outputs, enhancing semantic understanding at the expense of increased computational resources [5].

- **Trade-offs:** More sophisticated reranking models yield better relevance and coherence but incur higher latency and operational costs. Simpler reranking may be faster but less precise, affecting downstream generation quality [1][5].

## Introduction

Enterprise search systems are increasingly adopting retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) frameworks to meet the rising demand for precise, contextually relevant information retrieval combined with natural language generation. RAG systems leverage large language models (LLMs) that generate responses based on retrieved document snippets, making the quality of retrieval and document chunking critical to overall performance [1].

A core challenge in RAG is effectively segmenting large documents into manageable chunks that preserve semantic coherence while enabling efficient retrieval. The choice of chunking strategy directly impacts retrieval accuracy, response relevance, and computational costs. ## Chunking Strategies in RAG Systems

### Fixed-Size Chunking

Fixed-size chunking involves splitting documents into uniform segments, typically based on character or token counts. This approach is computationally efficient and straightforward to implement, making it suitable for high-throughput environments [3]. However, it often results in segments that cut across sentence boundaries or semantic units, potentially degrading retrieval relevance and coherence.

### Semantic and Structure-Aware Chunking

Semantic chunking aims to preserve meaningful units, such as paragraphs or sections, by leveraging natural language processing (NLP) techniques like sentence boundary detection or topic segmentation [4]. Hierarchical chunking further organizes content into nested structures, facilitating more contextually rich retrieval. Metadata-enriched chunking incorporates additional contextual information—such as document metadata or semantic labels—to enhance retrieval precision [5].

While these methods improve recall and relevance, they introduce increased computational complexity and engineering overhead. They often require sophisticated NLP pipelines and domain-specific tuning, which may impact scalability.

### Recursive Character Splitting

Recursive character splitting with moderate overlap is a hybrid approach that balances efficiency and semantic coherence. It recursively divides text into chunks based on character limits, with overlaps to maintain context continuity [6]. This method is regarded as a strong default in many enterprise applications due to its simplicity and effectiveness in preserving semantic integrity without excessive computational costs.

### Trade-offs Summary

| Strategy | Pros | Cons |
|------------|-------|-------|
| Fixed-size | Fast, simple, scalable | May split sentences, reduce relevance |
| Semantic/Hierarchical | Better coherence, relevance | Higher complexity, slower processing |
| Metadata-enriched | Context-aware, precise | Additional engineering overhead |
| Recursive splitting | Balanced, default choice | Slightly more complex than fixed-size |

## Reranking Models and Trade-offs

### List-Aware Reranking

List-aware reranking models evaluate and reorder entire lists of retrieved documents based on relevance scores, diversity, or other criteria [7]. These models often employ neural networks trained to optimize ranking metrics directly, such as NDCG or MAP. They are effective in improving the relevance of top results but can be computationally intensive, especially with large candidate pools.

### Neural Joint Models

Neural joint reranking models integrate retrieval and reranking into a unified framework, often leveraging transformer architectures to jointly encode documents and queries [8]. These models can better capture complex relevance signals and contextual dependencies, leading to improved accuracy. However, their high computational cost and latency may limit deployment in real-time enterprise systems.

### Trade-offs Summary

| Model Type | Pros | Cons |
|--------------|-------|-------|
| List-aware | Effective, flexible | Computationally intensive |
| Neural joint | Deep contextual understanding | High latency, resource demands |

For instance, high-throughput systems may favor fixed-size chunking combined with efficient list-aware rerankers, while mission-critical applications might benefit from semantic chunking and neural reranking despite higher costs.
