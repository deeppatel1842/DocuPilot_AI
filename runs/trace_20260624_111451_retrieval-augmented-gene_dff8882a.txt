RAG combines retrieval systems with LLMs to generate contextually grounded responses, mitigating hallucination and improving factual accuracy [1]. Enterprise search benefits from RAG by enabling domain-specific knowledge integration, but faces challenges such as document heterogeneity, long documents exceeding LLM context windows, and the need for precise retrieval [2].

### 2.2 Chunking Strategies

Chunking divides documents into manageable segments for retrieval and LLM input. Common strategies include:

- **Fixed-Size Sliding Window:** Segments text into fixed-length chunks, often overlapping, to cover content exhaustively but risks splitting semantically coherent units [3].
- **Recursive Chunking:** Leverages document hierarchy to create nested chunks aligned with logical sections, balancing chunk size and semantic integrity [4].
- **Breakpoint-Based Semantic Chunking:** Uses NLP techniques to detect topic shifts or paragraph boundaries, aiming to preserve semantic coherence [5].
- **Structure-Aware Chunking:** Incorporates explicit document metadata and structural elements (e.g., headers, lists) to form chunks that respect document organization, improving retrieval precision and efficiency [6].

### 2.3 Reranking Approaches

Reranking refines initial retrieval results to improve relevance before LLM generation. Approaches include:

- **Separate Reranking and Truncation Pipelines:** Traditional two-stage methods that rerank retrieved documents and then truncate for LLM input [7].
- **Joint Reranking-Truncation Models (e.g., GenRT):** Simultaneously optimize reranking and truncation using list-level contextual features, reducing error propagation and improving retrieval quality [8].
- **Hybrid Multi-Stage Pipelines:** Combine sparse retrieval (e.g., BM25), dense embeddings, and cross-encoder reranking to enhance precision and downstream generation accuracy [9].

### 2.4 Challenges in Enterprise Contexts

Enterprise documents often include complex formats such as piping and instrumentation diagrams (P&IDs), tables, and domain-specific metadata, complicating chunking and retrieval [6]. Additionally, computational constraints and latency requirements necessitate efficient chunking and reranking strategies [10].
